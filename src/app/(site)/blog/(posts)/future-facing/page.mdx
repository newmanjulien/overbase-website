## AI has improved at breakneck speeds for decades

Google Translate is a stack of multiple breakthroughs. AI-driven translation was unreliable 15 years ago. Now it delivers near-perfect translations for most languages.

OCR barely worked 15 years ago. Today Google Translate can read text perfectly, fast, and free.

Gong is another example. The AI-driven call transcription tech that powers their product was not reliable 15 years ago.

Search is the most recent example.

Amazon-style search used to be the cutting edge of AI search. Later, GPT-based search got good enough that our last business, Dedicated.ai, used it to find sales signals and turn them into pre-packaged emails. ## Search is the most recent breakthrough

Amazon-style search used to be the cutting edge of AI search.

Later, GPT-based search got so good that our last business, Dedicated.ai, used it to find sales signals and turn them into pre-packaged emails.

Today GPT search is good enough that my mom uses it instead of Google. And I use it instead of Stack Overflow.

But it's not because search is more useful than OCR that the improvements in search are more technically impressive.

## And AI has been the main driver of tech market cap

AI has also driven a tremendous amount of commercial outcomes. The single greatest driver of tech market cap in the past decade is likely AI-optimized targeting.

It's likely that Facebook's business would have gone down the drain without AI-optimized ad targeting when Apple increased privacy.

But it's not just Facebook. Google's market cap has mostly been created by better ROI driven by AI targeting. Netflix's entire product is about building AI-driven user profiles to target movies at you. Same for Amazon who use AI-driven targeting to get consumers to buy stuff they don't need.

## AI isn't accelerating it's just continuing to improve fast

The fact that non-technical people are just now noticing these outcomes doesn't mean the tech is advancing faster than before.

GPT-5 is more useful than GPT-3. Using GPT-5 for coding is night-and-day compared to GPT-3.

But GPT-5 is more of a refinement that applies similar LLM tech in the real world, and less of a technical leap.

The truth is that AI tech has been improving fast for decades, and it continues to improve fast. World models for example will likely be a huge leap forward in a few years just like LLMs were.

But AI tech isn't actually improving faster at all. Some could argue that the tech is improving a tad faster, but barely and definitely not in proportion to the amount of additional investment going in.

## Founders need to live in truth

We can assume AI will continue to improve fast. Like it has for several decades. And we can assume that those improvements will compound.

But we cannot build businesses that assume the pace of improvement between 2030 and 2040 will be faster than between 2010 and 2020. The data points the other way.

And we cannot build businesses that assume that a technology that can't reliably book a flight is somehow going to replace all knowledge work in 2 years.

## The AI startup playbook

The way to build the future is known. It's the same way AI startups have always built the future. It's a unique playbook that is different from the software playbook because AI is not software.

The AI playbook is simple: start with a human service, then convert it to AI when the tech catches up.

Our last business Dedicated.ai for example started as a human service. And it became fully automated with GPT-2.

## Automation arrives in two phases, not progressively

We got the counter-intuitive part of the playbook wrong at Dedicated.ai. The counterintuitive part is that automation happens in two distinct phases.

For years, we could not automate our human service while keeping quality high. Despite great engineers and lots of training data.

Then it suddenly got easy when the tech caught up, and all the engineering work we'd done was obsolete.

## What the early years are really about

As AI founders, we spend the first 5 years creating impossible-to-ignore value and building defensibility.

The metrics that matter are NRR and ACV. We are not creating sophisticated tech, and we are not worrying about margins.

If we are, we are building the past.

This doesn't mean that founders need to forget about the end game. In fact, strategy matters more than ever for AI startups.

## 10x SaaS budgets

The real opportunity for AI startups is to capture budgets that used to go to salaries. Which lets us get so much bigger than SaaS ever did.

To get human-level budgets, you have to start by selling a human service.

That means the valuation of an AI-only product should be SaaS-level. While the valuation of a human service that will later become AI should be 10x higher. Because the human service's future revenue will be an order of magnitude larger.

## Future-facing starts low tech

We need to get used to the ideas that building fancy tech is backwards-facing and valuation-killing. While being low-tech is future-facing and valuation-driving.

This is hard for people who spent their careers in software to internalize. Because it's the opposite of the software startup playbook.

It's also impossible for legacy vendors to execute on. Because it's the opposite of their business model.

But it's the entrepreneurs who execute on AI-native playbooks who will own the entire future.

And that's pretty much nobody right now. Which means that founders have a unique opportunity in this short moment in time to build history's most valuable businesses. With close to 0 competition.
